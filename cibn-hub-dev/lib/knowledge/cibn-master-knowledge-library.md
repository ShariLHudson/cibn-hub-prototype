1) Invite up to ~1,000 people to a LinkedIn event (Connection
Extension method)
Why it matters
• If you only post the event link, most people won’t see it.
• Inviting your existing connections puts the event in front of real humans who already know
you (or at least are connected).
• If you want consistent event attendance without paying for ads, inviting + follow-up is the
fastest lever.
Step-by-step: create or open your LinkedIn event
• Go to LinkedIn.
• Click My Network.
• On the left side, click Events.
• Click Create.
• Add your event image, title, description, date/time, and location details (if in-person).
• Add a registration link if you want to collect contact details (recommended).
• Choose event type:
• In-person
• • Add event location and details, and include any signup link you need.
• Online
• • Prefer a registration link over posting a raw meeting-room link publicly to reduce
unwanted drop-ins.
• LinkedIn Audio/Live-style event
• • Know that some formats are more “audio broadcast” style, with limited screen
sharing.
Step-by-step: invite people using the Connection Extension
• Open the event page.
• If needed, click Attend (you must be marked as attending to invite/share).
• Click Share or Invite (wording may vary).
• When the invitation window appears, use the Connection Extension’s auto-scroll / auto-load
feature to populate invitees.
• If it’s an in-person event, you can filter by location to target people in that city/area.
• Click Stop when you’ve loaded the amount you want (LinkedIn limits may apply).
• Click Invite to send invitations in bulk.
Key notes a GPT should remember
• This method invites your existing LinkedIn connections (first-degree).
• LinkedIn has rate limits (example: some accounts may hit weekly invite caps). Always
advise users to stay within LinkedIn’s limits to avoid account restrictions.
• For online events, inviting globally is fine. For local events, location filtering helps
attendance.

2) Follow up after invites so people actually register and attend
Why it matters
• When someone clicks “Attend/Interested,” it often means “maybe.” It is not the same as
registering on your landing page.
• A short follow-up message turns “hand raised” into “registered and showing up.”
Timing
• Start follow-up about 24 hours after invitations go out.
Step-by-step: find who to message
• Open the event page.
• Find the section that shows attendees/interested people (often under a “Networking” or
attendee list view).
• Focus on your first-degree connections (the people you can message directly).
• Second- and third-degree attendees may not have a message button. Don’t spam connect
requests.
Follow-up message templates (simple + effective)
Template A - Offer to register them (often boosts conversion)
• Hi [Name] - I saw you’re interested in [Event Name]. Want me to register you? All I need is
the best email to use.
Template B - Send registration link
• Hi [Name] - I saw you’re interested in [Event Name]. Here’s the registration link: [short
link]. Hope to see you there!
Batching the work (fast)
• Copy/paste your best follow-up message to each first-degree attendee who clicked yes.
• Keep a short link ready (e.g., a branded short link) so you can paste it quickly.
Team strategy (big attendance booster)
• Work with 3-10 collaborators.
• Each collaborator invites from their own connections (within LinkedIn limits).
• Then each collaborator follows up with their first-degree “yes” responders.
• Result: a much fuller event without ads.

3) 10 best things you can do to your LinkedIn profile (so invites
convert better)
Why it matters
• Your profile is like a free extra page on your website.
• Invites and messages convert better when your profile looks complete, clear, and
professional.
• Fill out your full profile. Book 30-60 minutes and complete missing sections.
• Add a professional headshot. Clear, business-appropriate photo. Avoid cropped group
photos or casual selfies.
• Add a backdrop (banner) image. Use a city image, brand graphic, or offer-based banner.
Don’t leave it blank.
• Sync your email contacts. Connect past contacts so your network grows faster (how-to
steps may vary by LinkedIn version).
• Add connections from real-world contacts. Convert business cards and past contacts into
LinkedIn connections (when appropriate).
• Join large groups. Join groups where your target market hangs out and where conversation
is allowed.
• Write an article. Articles act like extra “pages” people can read to understand what you do.
• Put phone + email in your About section. Make it easy for people to contact you (especially
from Google search snippets).
• Post a signature video. Add a short “who I am + what I do” video to your profile. Posting
video directly to LinkedIn can improve views.
• Make positive posts daily. Comment with words (not only likes). Consistency builds visibility
and trust.

5) Booked-appointments messaging workflow (no spam)
What problem this solves
• Many people connect on LinkedIn but never follow through.
• Spammy “here’s my link” messages damage relationships and get ignored.
• A question-based conversation approach can turn connections into real appointments.
Core rule: don’t lead with a link
• Avoid opening messages like: “Here’s my event/link - click now.”
• Instead: ask a simple question, confirm interest, then offer the next step.
Connection request message (simple)
• Send a friendly, low-pressure connection message (short and human).
• Goal: get the connection accepted first.
Conversation starter (question-based)
• After they connect, ask a question related to why you’re reaching out.
• Example structure:
• 1) Context
• • “I noticed…” / “We were both in…” / “I work with…”
• 2) One question
• • “Are you currently looking to…?” / “What are you focused on this quarter?”
• 3) Soft next step
• • “If it’s useful, I can share details or we can do a quick call.”
Turning curiosity into an appointment
• If they ask detailed questions, reply with: “Great question - we should hop on a quick call so
I can give you the best answer.”
• Share your calendar link only after they show interest.
• Goal: pre-qualify and book (not teach everything in DMs).
Follow-through list (easy win)
• If you attended a networking meeting and saved the chat list, you can message those
attendees afterward.
• Message: “We met at [event]. Want to book a quick 1:1?”
• This prevents “I meant to follow up but never did.”
Spam signals to avoid
• Messaging people with the wrong context (clearly automated and not specific).
• Pitching immediately after connecting without a real conversation.
• Copy/paste that doesn’t match what your profile says you do.

bulletPeople who rely on LinkedIn for networking, referrals, and lead generation.

Why this matters (benefits)

bulletMore registrations means you collect contact info (email list + CRM), not just LinkedIn “hands raised.”

C. Follow up flow (turn “Interested” into “Registered”)
Based on the follow up transcript: message the people who said “yes/interested” and are in your
1st degree connections.
Core idea
bulletA LinkedIn “Attend” click is like someone raising their hand—it is not a registration.
bulletYour goal is to re engage them and move them to your registration link (landing page) so you capture
contact info.
When to follow up
bulletStart follow ups about 24 hours after sending invites.
bulletRepeat follow ups later in the week as needed (especially for longer lead times).
How to find who to message
bulletOpen the LinkedIn event page.
bulletUse the event’s attendee/interest area (often shown as networking/attendees).
bulletFocus on 1st degree connections because you can message them directly.
Copy paste message templates (GPT ready)
1 Option 1 (direct link):
Hi {FirstName} — I saw you marked “Interested” in {EventName}. Here’s the quick registration link so
you’re fully registered: {RegLink}.
2 Option 2 (offer to register them):
Hi {FirstName} — I saw you’re interested in {EventName}. Want me to register you? If yes, just send me
the best email address to use.
3 Option 3 (short + friendly):
Hey {FirstName}! Thanks for the interest in {EventName}. Here’s the signup link: {RegLink}. Hope to
see you there!
Why the “offer to register you” line works
bullet It removes friction (they don’t have to click anything).
bullet In the transcript, this approach was described as increasing conversions (results will vary).
bulletYou still capture the email so they can receive confirmations and reminders.
Team strategy (multiply results)
bulletDo invites + follow ups with collaborators.
bulletExample workflow:
bullet3–10 people each send invites to their own connections (within platform limits).
bulletLater in the week, each person messages their own 1st degree “Interested” contacts to push
registration.
