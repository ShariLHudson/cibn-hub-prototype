# CIBN — Follow-Ups That Work
## Overview
This file covers the full networking sales process — from first touch to closed deal — including how to use funnels, run one-on-ones, deliver proposals, and ask for referrals. The AI uses this content to coach users on building a real sales process with scripts, tracking, and consistent follow-up that converts prospects into buyers and referral sources.

---

## Why This Matters
Most business owners do not have a sales process mapped out. They wing it — and winging it means no data, no improvement, and no scale.

Before the pandemic, it took 7–14 touches to convert a prospect into a buyer. Today it takes **30–60 touches**. Without a system, that is impossible to manage. With a process, it becomes achievable and repeatable.

👉 Sales is not an event. It is a process.

---

## The Core Principle: Script Everything
Every step in the sales process should be scripted. If you change your approach every time, you have no way to know what is working.

A script can be:
- Tracked
- Tweaked
- Duplicated by others

Without scripts, you cannot scale your business or ever hire a salesperson who can replicate your results.

## Understanding Funnels
A sales funnel is a series of steps that moves a person from first contact to becoming a buyer. Each step filters out those who are not ready to buy while moving qualified prospects forward.

**Example — Facebook Funnel:**
1,000 people take a free offer → 500 join a challenge → 350 enter the group → 180 watch daily content → 12 sales at $2,000 = $24,000 revenue on $4,000 in ad spend.

**Example — Webinar Funnel:**
1,000 click a Google ad → 300 register → 100 attend → 10 close on the spot → 12 more close in follow-up over the following weeks.

Tracking your funnel data tells you:
- Which funnel is converting better
- Where prospects are dropping off
- Your cost per lead and cost per sale
- When it's time to scale up

## The Three Networking Funnels
Networking produces three separate funnels, each operating at the same time:

### 1. Sales Funnel
These are potential direct buyers — people who are in your target market and have the budget to purchase from you.

### 2. Referral Funnel
These are people who will not buy from you directly but can send you one or more warm referrals. A warm referral from a trusted person closes at twice the rate of a cold lead.

### 3. Collaborating Partner Funnel
These are people whose clients are your clients — but they sell something different. A strong Collaborating Partner can send you 3–5 qualified leads every single month. Their referrals come pre-sold by a trusted advisor and close at approximately 80%.

**At every one-on-one, you are sorting prospects into one of these three silos.** The questions you ask determine which funnel they belong in.

---

## Step 1 — The Invitation (Top of Funnel)
Getting someone to book a one-on-one with you is the first step in all three funnels.

- People who heard your infomercial but didn't book are impressions, not leads
- People who booked a one-on-one are at the top of your funnel
- Track how many appointments you book each week — this number drives everything downstream

**Goal:** Book 50 one-on-ones per month (approx. 12–13 per week across your meetings).

---

## Step 2 — The One-on-One Meeting
The one-on-one is your qualifying step. Its purpose is NOT to make a sale on the spot.

**Three goals of every one-on-one:**
1. Determine if this person is a potential buyer, a referral source, or a Collaborating Partner
2. Build the know, like, and trust relationship
3. Find ways to add value to them and their business

**How to run it:**
- Spend the first 15 minutes learning about them: who they are, what they sell, who their target market is
- Then share who you are, what you do, and why you're great — give a compelling tip, but don't give away the full solution
- Ask questions. The person asking the most questions is in control of the conversation

**At the end, determine their silo:**
- Potential buyer → book a sales presentation
- Not a buyer → shift to referral questions
- Strong alignment with your target market → explore Collaborating Partner potential

**Important:** If they are a potential buyer, do NOT give away your full product or service information in the one-on-one. If they already know everything, there's no reason to book the next appointment. Keep it light — book the sales presentation.

Also ask: Are they a paid member of the networking group? Members build longer-term relationships, refer more consistently, and are more invested in your success. Encourage guests to join.

---

## Step 3 — The Sales Presentation
The sales presentation is its own dedicated appointment — not something squeezed into the last 15 minutes of a one-on-one.

**Script for the sales presentation:**
1. Ask questions first — understand their situation before offering solutions
2. Offer 1–2 high-value solutions to demonstrate you know what you're doing
3. If the product or service is **under $500** — do the full presentation and go for the close now
4. If the product or service is **over $500** — offer to take their information back and create a customized proposal; book the proposal appointment on the spot

**Closing:**
- Closing means asking for the sale and being ready to accept payment
- Not everyone says yes the first time — ask for the sale at least **three times**, in three different ways
- If they agree to a proposal meeting, they are genuinely interested — people don't show up for proposals they don't want

**Rule:** Book the next appointment before leaving the current one. If you leave without booking, it's as if the meeting never happened.

---

## Step 4 — The Proposal
The proposal is a formal document presented in person or via Zoom.

**Critical rule: Never send the proposal in advance.** Once they have it, there is no reason to meet. Sending it early kills more sales than any other single action.

**How to deliver it:**
- Bring a printed or screen-shared document
- Open with a 3-sentence summary: remind them of the problem they identified, then present your customized solution
- Include fast facts and statistics — but keep it concise (remember Choleric personalities want the bottom line quickly)
- Be prepared to accept payment that day

**Closing rate:** 80% of prospects who show up for a proposal meeting will buy — if you are ready to receive payment and your process is solid. If they're not closing, review and rehearse the steps with a colleague.

---

## Step 5 — The Follow-Up
The follow-up is where most salespeople lose the sale — and where professionals make their money.

**If they bought:**
- Follow up while they are still in the honeymoon stage
- Ask for referrals immediately — this is the highest-value moment to ask
- Move them into the Referral Funnel process

**If they did not buy:**
- Book the follow-up appointment before leaving the proposal meeting — never say "I'll get back to you"
- Bring new, relevant information to the follow-up
- Ask for the sale **three times** in three different ways
- If they say "consider it in the spring," book the spring meeting now
- If they still don't buy — ask for referrals anyway, and book the next follow-up

👉 The gold is in the follow-up. You are three feet from the gold. Keep going.

## Asking for Referrals — The Referral Funnel
Most networkers get no referrals because they don't have a referral process. Casually asking once at the end of a meeting rarely works.

**The professional approach:**
- Create a list of 10 specific questions that help the other person think of someone in your target market
- Read the questions aloud — do not hand the list and ask them to get back to you
- Hand them the paper and a pen, and ask them to write the **first name** of whoever comes to mind as you read each question
- Once done, ask for the last name of each person
- Explain clearly how you will reach out (you will mention their name, and that you'd appreciate an introduction)

**Why it works:** Specific questions trigger specific memories. Asking for ten referrals means you may get three, five, or ten — asking for one means you rarely get even one.

**Real result:** One insurance professional using this exact method regularly earned over $30,000 per quarter from a single networking meeting per week — while others in the same industry went months without a single sale.

**Write your 10 referral questions now.** Tailor them to your industry and your ideal client profile. Use them in every one-on-one where the person is not a direct buyer.

## The Collaboration Funnel
Collaborating Partners are not one-time referrers. They are strategic relationships where both parties serve the same target market but offer different products or services.

**What makes Collaborating Partners different:**
- Their clients are already buyers — not tire-kickers
- The referrals come pre-warmed by a trusted advisor
- Closing ratio is approximately 80% (vs. 1 in 5 for standard networking leads)
- One strong Collaborating Partner can send 3–5 leads per month, every month

Building Collaborating Partner relationships takes time and intention, but it becomes the most predictable and low-effort source of new business over time.

## What the Full Funnel Produces (Example)
Starting with 50 one-on-one appointments in one month:

- 7 direct sales from networking contacts
- 8 sales from referrals generated in those meetings
- 2 sales from Collaborating Partner leads (higher value, easier to close)
- **Total: 17 sales at $2,000 = $34,000**

Increasing appointments increases results. Decreasing appointments decreases them. The math is trackable — which means it is improvable.

## Common Mistakes
- No sales process — winging every step
- Giving away too much information before booking the next appointment
- Sending the proposal before the meeting
- Not asking for the sale at least three times
- Leaving a meeting without booking the next one
- Asking for referrals casually instead of using a scripted process
- Only asking for one referral instead of ten
- Treating networking as relationship-building only, with no funnel tracking

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How many times should I ask for the sale?**
A: At least three times, in three different ways. Most people give up after one no — professionals keep going.

**Q: Should I send the proposal before the meeting?**
A: Never. Sending it early eliminates the reason for the meeting and kills the sale.

**Q: How many referrals should I ask for?**
A: Ask for ten. Specific, scripted questions that you read aloud while the other person has a pen in hand. You will rarely get less than three.

**Q: What if someone doesn't buy — is the meeting wasted?**
A: No. Shift to the Referral Funnel. Use your 10 referral questions. A warm referral closes at twice the rate of any cold lead.

**Q: How many one-on-ones should I be booking?**
A: Aim for 50 per month across your networking meetings. That is about 12–13 per week.
